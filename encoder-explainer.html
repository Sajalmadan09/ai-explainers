THE BASICS

What is each one, simply?

REGULAR ENCODER

The Translator

"Takes one thing, turns it into a number-code."

An Encoder's job is to understand and compress something — text, audio, an image — into a dense numerical representation (called an "embedding" or "vector") that a machine can work with.

It reads every single piece of input individually and produces a representation for each one. Like a translator who processes every word, one at a time.

AGGREGATION ENCODER

The Summarizer

"Groups many things, squeezes them into one code."

An Aggregation Encoder does everything a regular encoder does — but it takes multiple items at once and merges/summarizes them into a single, compact representation called a patch.

Instead of one code per item, you get one code for a group of items. Like a meeting summary instead of reading every email.

REAL WORLD ANALOGIES

Think of it like this

📸

Photos on your phone

Regular Encoder: Your phone backs up every single photo individually — one by one, each gets its own slot, its own label, its own upload. 1000 photos = 1000 separate operations.

Aggregation Encoder: Your phone groups photos from the same day into one album — "Saturday at the beach" — and stores the summary of that day. 1000 photos = maybe 20 albums. Way faster to browse!

📰

Reading the news

Regular Encoder: You read every single article, word by word, and write a one-line note for each one separately. If there are 100 articles, you write 100 notes.

Aggregation Encoder: You read a batch of articles on the same topic — say, 5 articles about the economy — and write one combined summary: "Markets fell due to inflation worries." You've captured the essence of 5 articles in 1 note.

🎵

Music (closest to DiTAR!)

Regular Encoder: Imagine transcribing a song note-by-note. Every 1/100th of a second of sound gets its own separate description. A 3-minute song = millions of individual descriptions. The AI has to process ALL of them.

Aggregation Encoder: Instead, you group every 10 notes into one "phrase" and describe the phrase. "Rising melody, high pitch, excited tone." That's an audio patch — a meaningful chunk, not a single millisecond. DiTAR works in these chunks, so the brain model only handles dozens of patches, not millions of raw frames.

🧑‍🍳

A sandwich shop order

Regular Encoder: The cashier writes down every single ingredient separately on the ticket: "bread", "cheese", "tomato", "lettuce", "mayo", "ham", "bread". 7 items on the ticket for one sandwich.

Aggregation Encoder: The cashier writes "1× Club Sandwich." One item on the ticket — but it carries all the same information, compactly. The kitchen knows exactly what to make.

SIDE BY SIDE

Complete comparison

ENCODER AGGREGATION ENCODER
INPUT One item at a time (one frame, one word, one pixel) A group/patch of multiple items at once
OUTPUT One embedding per input item One embedding per group (patch)
SEQUENCE LENGTH Long — same length as original input Short — compressed by patch size (e.g., 10×)
COMPUTATION Higher — model must process every single token Lower — model processes far fewer patches
DETAIL Very fine-grained detail preserved per token Some local detail averaged out, but structure preserved
USE IN BERT, ViT (images), speech classifiers DiTAR (speech), MAR (images), PatchTST (time series)
ANALOGY Reading every word in a book Reading the chapter summaries of a book
SPEED Slower at inference (long context) Much faster — shorter sequence = less attention cost
PRACTICAL GUIDE

When is each one used?

Regular Encoder — use when...

Every detail matters individually (e.g., classifying a specific word in a sentence)
You need exact token-level alignment (e.g., Named Entity Recognition, subtitles)
Your input is already short (a sentence, a small image)
You're doing tasks like sentiment analysis, translation, question answering
Examples: BERT for text, ViT patch encoder for images, Wav2Vec for speech features

Aggregation Encoder — use when...

Your raw input is very long (audio = thousands of frames, video, long docs)
Speed matters and you can afford to summarize local regions
You're pairing it with an expensive model (like a big LM) that can't handle long sequences
You want to generate sequences autoregressively (one patch at a time)
Examples: DiTAR for speech, Temporal Patch Embeddings for video, PatchTST for time series
⚡ THE CORE INSIGHT

Both encoders do the same fundamental job: turn raw data into numbers a model can understand. The difference is granularity. A regular encoder says "I'll describe every grain of sand." An aggregation encoder says "I'll describe every handful." DiTAR uses the aggregation approach because audio is like a beach — millions of grains — and no language model can afford to read them one by one.
